  At FEED/DEPT, I was embedded in the eBay North America account as a Digital/UX Designer, producing email campaigns across eBay's major verticals, handbags, watches, sneakers, and beyond. The work ran on a tight 2-week sprint cycle, requiring both creative consistency and the ability to adapt quickly to new briefs, products, and seasonal moments.

  Alongside my responsibilities on the eBay NA account was working alongside my manager to build and maintain the template library, over 26 distinct template types that the entire design team relied on. These weren't just aesthetic frameworks; they were production tools that had to be flexible enough for any product category while holding together visually across an enormous range of content.

conclusionHeading: The Design Thinking
conclusion: |
  Building a template system that works for a team is a fundamentally different problem than designing something for a single client. The templates had to be aesthetically flexible enough to feel fresh across seasonal moments and product categories, while being structurally consistent enough that a junior designer picking one up for the first time could use it well. Every element had to earn its place, not just visually, but functionally. Does it render at 320px? Does the hierarchy hold when images are off? Is the CTA immediately obvious to someone scrolling fast on a phone?

  Each sprint involved close collaboration with copywriters to establish clear information hierarchies, cut redundant content, and simplify client wireframes into clean, scannable layouts. Junior designers were mentored and given feedback on template usage and design decisions. Cross-team visual consistency was built out alongside other designers, including a cohesive blue winter holiday theme across the Handbags and Watches series. Original iconography was developed for seller-focused sections, creating visual shorthand that made dense information readable at a glance.

  The templates weren't meant to look like templates. They were meant to look like craft. Four years later, they still do.
